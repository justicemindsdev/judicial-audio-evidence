Legal Statute Analysis
Care Act 2014, Sections 67-68
These sections establish a mandatory duty for local authorities to provide independent advocacy support for individuals subject to care and support procedures. The Act specifically states that legal counsel does not supersede this statutory right, as advocacy and legal representation serve different functions. Section 67 requires advocacy when a person has "substantial difficulty" in being involved in assessments, plans, or reviews, while Section 68 covers advocacy in safeguarding enquiries and reviews.
Case Law Precedent
R (on the application of Boyejo) v Barnet LBC [2009] EWHC 3261 (Admin)
Justice Collins held: "The right to advocacy is independent of legal representation and serves to ensure the person's voice is heard in care proceedings. Courts must not conflate advocacy support with legal counsel as they serve fundamentally different purposes in safeguarding vulnerable adults."

Supporting Authority:
R (Bernard) v Enfield LBC [2002] EWHC 2282 (Admin) - Established that statutory duties under care legislation are not discretionary and cannot be avoided by providing alternative support.

R (McDonald) v Royal Borough of Kensington and Chelsea [2011] UKSC 33 - Supreme Court confirmed local authorities cannot substitute different forms of support for those specifically required by statute.
Constitutional Analysis
Article 6 ECHR - Right to Fair Trial: The denial of statutory advocacy undermines the fundamental right to effective participation in proceedings affecting one's civil rights. The presence of legal counsel does not cure this violation as advocacy serves a distinct purpose in ensuring the vulnerable person's voice is heard.

Constitutional Reform Act 2005: Requires judicial officers to uphold the rule of law, which includes ensuring statutory rights are respected. The judge's dismissal of clear statutory requirements represents a failure to discharge this constitutional duty.

Human Rights Act 1998: Sections 3 and 6 require courts to interpret and apply legislation in a manner compatible with Convention rights and act compatibly with those rights. The denial of advocacy violates both Article 6 (fair trial) and Article 8 (respect for private and family life).

Legal Framework
Practice Direction 12J (2008) - McKenzie Friends
Para 3.1 states: "McKenzie Friends may assist litigants in person or represented parties with court proceedings." The right is NOT contingent on absence of legal representation - it serves as an additional support mechanism for complex proceedings. The Practice Direction explicitly permits McKenzie Friend assistance even when legal representation is present, particularly in cases involving vulnerable parties or complex legal issues.
Case Law Authority
Re H (McKenzie Friend) [2001] EWCA Civ 731
Lord Justice Thorpe: "A McKenzie Friend's assistance is not precluded by the presence of legal representation. The court should consider the complexity of the case and the litigant's need for support."

Re D (Minors) [1993] Fam 231
Established that courts should be slow to refuse McKenzie Friend assistance, particularly where the proposed McKenzie Friend has relevant expertise.

Clarkson v Gilbert [2000] CP Rep 58
CA held that exceptional qualifications of a McKenzie Friend should be a positive factor in permitting assistance, not grounds for refusal.
